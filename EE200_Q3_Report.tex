\section{Question 3A: Sonic Signatures (Acoustic Fingerprinting)}
%====================================================

\subsection{Problem Statement}
The objective of this project is to build a robust, Shazam-style audio fingerprinting system capable of identifying highly degraded, short audio snippets (queries) by matching them against a database of 50 full-length tracks. The algorithm must exhibit near-perfect accuracy, resilience to environmental noise, and a constant-time $O(1)$ lookup complexity to ensure instant identification.

\subsection{Mathematical Foundation \& Signal Processing Pipeline}

\subsubsection{1. Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT)}
Audio is inherently non-stationary; its frequency content changes rapidly over time. To capture this frequency evolution, we compute the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT). The signal $x[n]$ is partitioned into overlapping frames by multiplying it with a sliding window function $w[n]$ (specifically, a Hann window to minimize spectral leakage):
\begin{equation}
    X[m, k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n + mH] \cdot w[n] \cdot e^{-j2\pi k n / N}
\end{equation}
where $H$ is the hop length and $N$ is the FFT size. The resulting 2D complex matrix is converted into a log-magnitude spectrogram $S[m,k] = 20 \log_{10}(|X[m,k]|)$.

\begin{math_box}[Time-Frequency Uncertainty Principle]
    By the Gabor Limit, we cannot simultaneously achieve perfect time and perfect frequency resolution. 
    \begin{align*}
        \text{Short Window } (N \text{ small}) &\implies \text{High Time Res, Poor Freq Res} \\
        \text{Long Window } (N \text{ large}) &\implies \text{Poor Time Res, High Freq Res}
    \end{align*}
    For music at a sample rate of $11,025$ Hz, choosing $N=2048$ with a hop length of $512$ provides a balanced $46$ ms window, yielding a frequency bin resolution of roughly $5.4$ Hz.
\end{math_box}

\subsubsection{2. Constellation Map \& Peak Extraction}
Raw spectrograms contain millions of data points and are highly susceptible to noise. A matching algorithm comparing raw matrices would fail if the timing was misaligned by even a fraction of a second. Instead, we compress the audio into a sparse \textit{constellation map}.

A cell $X[m,k]$ is designated as a peak if it is the strict local maximum within a defined 2D neighborhood (e.g., $15 \times 15$ cells in the time-frequency domain). This effectively isolates the dominant harmonic frequencies and sharp percussive transients, reducing a dense spectrogram into a sparse set of coordinates $(t, f)$ representing less than 1\% of the original data.

\subsubsection{3. Combinatorial Hashing (Anchor-Target Pairing)}
\begin{insight_box}[Single Peaks vs. Paired Hashes]
If we attempt to index a song using single frequencies $f_1$, the system will generate massive amounts of false positives (e.g., thousands of songs share a 440 Hz 'A' note). However, by pairing an \textit{anchor peak} $f_1$ with a \textit{target peak} $f_2$ separated by time $\Delta t$, we create a highly specific hash: $(f_1, f_2, \Delta t)$. 

The combinatorial probability of two different songs containing the exact same two frequencies separated by the exact same microsecond time delta is exponentially lower. This pairing mechanism is the core reason the algorithm is cryptographic and noise-robust.
\end{insight_box}

\subsubsection{4. Database Indexing and Offset Histograms}
For every hash generated from the full songs, we store an entry in a hash map:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Database\_Map}\left[\, (f_1, f_2, \Delta t) \,\right] = [\, (\text{Song\_ID}, t_{\text{anchor}}) \,]
\end{equation}
When querying an unknown audio clip, we generate its hashes and look them up in the database in $O(1)$ time. For every matched hash, we calculate the relative time offset:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta t_{\text{offset}} = t_{\text{database\_anchor}} - t_{\text{query\_anchor}}
\end{equation}
If the song is a genuine match, the matched hashes will have maintained their relative timing, causing thousands of hits to agree on the exact same $\Delta t_{\text{offset}}$. A histogram of these offsets is plotted, and the peak of the histogram directly determines the match confidence score.

\subsection{Experiments, Observations, and Analysis}

\subsubsection{Experiment 1: The Window Length Trade-off}
To understand the Gabor Limit practically, we computed the STFT of an identical audio clip using two extreme window sizes: $N=256$ (short) and $N=4096$ (long).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Q3_outputs/02_window_length.png}
    \caption{STFT spectrograms comparing $N=256$ (left) against $N=4096$ (right).}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Observation \& Explanation:}\\
In the left plot ($N=256$), the vertical lines (representing percussive drum hits) are incredibly sharp, demonstrating excellent time resolution. However, the horizontal bands (musical notes) are smeared and thick, indicating poor frequency resolution. 

In the right plot ($N=4096$), the horizontal notes are razor-thin (excellent frequency resolution), but the sharp drum hits have been blurred horizontally across time, causing pre-echo artifacts (poor time localization). This demonstrates that $N=2048$ is the optimal ``Goldilocks'' trade-off for capturing both the rhythmic and melodic constellation peaks required for accurate hashing.

\vspace{0.5cm}

\subsubsection{Experiment 2: Single Peaks vs. Paired Hashes}
\textbf{Observation \& Explanation:}\\
When we attempted identification by simply recording the absolute timestamps of single high-energy peaks, the system failed entirely when the query clip was subjected to random cropping. An absolute timestamp is meaningless unless the audio starts at $t=0$. 

By transitioning to \textbf{paired hashes} $(f_1, f_2, \Delta t)$, the system becomes completely invariant to cropping. The time difference $\Delta t$ between two peaks is preserved regardless of where the clip was sliced from the original track. This relative structural encoding is what enables the offset histogram to reliably align the query with the database track.

\subsubsection{Experiment 3: Robustness to Additive Noise (AWGN)}
To test the resilience of the constellation map, we injected high-variance Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN) into a clean query clip, simulating a noisy café environment.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Q3_outputs/03_noise.png}
    \caption{Clean spectrogram (left) vs. Spectrogram heavily corrupted by AWGN (right).}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Observation \& Explanation:}\\
The right spectrogram visually demonstrates a vastly elevated noise floor (indicated by the brighter background). However, the fundamental algorithm succeeds despite this corruption. Why? Because the STFT concentrates the energy of pure tones into dense, isolated spikes. Even when broad-spectrum noise is added, the high-energy harmonic peaks remain the \textit{local maxima} within their neighborhoods. Since our algorithm only hashes the highest amplitude peaks and aggressively discards the rest of the spectrogram, the mathematical ``skeleton'' of the song is preserved, allowing for successful matches even at SNR levels approaching $0$ dB.

\vspace{0.5cm}

\subsubsection{Experiment 4: Pitch Shifting and Time Stretching}
We modified the query clip by applying a pitch shift of +4 semitones (without altering the tempo) to observe the effect on the hashing logic.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Q3_outputs/04_pitch_shift.png}
    \caption{Original spectrogram (left) vs. Pitch-shifted by +4 semitones (right).}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Observation \& Explanation:}\\
As seen on the right, the entire harmonic structure has shifted upward on the frequency axis. \textbf{The standard Shazam algorithm fails completely under pitch-shifting or time-stretching.} Because the algorithm relies on exact frequency bins $f_1$ and $f_2$ to form a hash key, shifting the pitch alters every single frequency, causing zero hashes to match the database.

\textbf{Proposed Algorithmic Fix:}\\
To make the system invariant to pitch shifts, one must alter the hashing function to use the \textbf{ratio} of frequencies instead of absolute frequencies. A pitch shift multiplies all frequencies by a constant factor $k$. Thus, the new hash key $\frac{k \cdot f_2}{k \cdot f_1}$ mathematically reduces to $\frac{f_2}{f_1}$, remaining perfectly invariant to pitch alterations. Similarly, for time-stretching, hashing the ratio of time deltas between a triplet of peaks would provide temporal invariance.

\subsection{Architecting for Cloud Constraints}
Streamlit Community Cloud and GitHub impose strict storage limitations (100MB per repository/file). Uploading the raw 394MB \texttt{.mp3} database was impossible. To bypass this and guarantee that the indexed song database ships with the deployed app so it works immediately, the following hybrid architecture was engineered:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pre-Computed Visuals (Library View):} Instead of calculating STFTs on the fly for the library browser, the acoustic fingerprint visuals (spectrograms and constellation maps) were pre-generated as lightweight \texttt{.png} images and stored in the \texttt{library\_images/} directory.
    \item \textbf{Pre-Computed Audio (Sample Auto-Generator):} To allow users to test the identification system without needing to manually upload a song, a script extracted 15-second lightweight \texttt{.wav} snippets from all 50 songs, storing them in \texttt{sample\_audio/}. This bypasses the need for the heavy MP3s.
    \item \textbf{Serialized Database:} The core algorithmic matching relies entirely on the \texttt{song\_db.pkl} hash map. This serialized dictionary contains over 3 million unique hashes but is highly compressed, shipping perfectly within the size limits.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Application Modes}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Single-Clip Identification Mode:} Users can upload a custom, degraded audio clip or test an internal sample. The app instantly processes the STFT, extracts peaks, queries the database in $O(1)$ time, and visualizes the intermediate steps: the downsampled Waveform, Spectrogram, Constellation Map, and the critical Offset Histogram that determines the final match confidence.
    \item \textbf{Batch Benchmark Mode:} Evaluators can utilize the ``Run Internal Auto-Benchmark'' feature. The app autonomously iterates over the pre-generated test clips, runs the fingerprinting algorithm in bulk, calculates system accuracy, and produces the strictly formatted \texttt{results.csv} containing the exact \texttt{filename, prediction} columns required for automated grading.
\end{enumerate}
